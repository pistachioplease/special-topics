What is Information Architecture?

Richard Saul Wurman was the first to coin the term information architecture at the American Institute of Architecture conference of 1976.

It is the process of arranging parts of something to make it understandable … basically to organize stuff.

IA focuses on the structural design of information or content.

IA can be relatively simple with a small project, and incredibly complicated with a large project.

IA Heuristics

  • Findable: is information located easily?
  • Accessible: is it consistent across all platforms?
  • Clear: is it easy to understand?
  • Communicative: is it informative and does it help a user complete tasks?
  • Usable: is the user able to complete tasks with no frustration?

IA Heuristics

  • Credible: is it worthy of confidence and reliable?
  • Controllable: is the user able to tailor information within their context and easily fix errors?
  • Valuable: is it desirable and improve customer satisfaction?
  • Learnable: is it able to be easily understood and used?
  • Delightful: how are user expectations exceeded?

General Guidelines for Drawing Site Maps

  • Just because it looks simple, doesn’t mean it makes sense.
  • Keep it clear and readable.
  • We usually draw top-to-bottom, not left-to-right.
  • Don’t make a Site Map “fancy”. It’s a technical document, not a fashion show.

Common Myth

You might hear people say that everything should be “3 clicks away” at all times. That means they learned UX in the 90’s and haven’t learned anything new since then. Focus on the user instead. Make sure they understand where they are, and where they can go, at all times. If your navigation is easy and clear, the number of clicks is irrelevant.

User Stories & Types of Information Architecture

User Story

Describes one possible path a user can take on your site or app.

It should be short, but complete.

You will need many user stories to describe your whole design.

A basic user story for Google.com might look like this

The user arrives on the main search page.

The user can enter any search query and submit with the mouse or keyboard.

The next page displays a list of search results with the most relevant results on top.

The user can click the link to go to the appropriate site, or they can navigate through more pages of results until they find something useful.

Purpose of stories is to describe flows, not how to specifically solve or design those actions.

If the flows are simple and effective, you are doing a good job (so far).

The structure of your pages determines the steps in your user stories. And to structure your pages you have to pick a type of Information Architecture to work with.

Types of Information Architecture

  • Categories
  • Tasks
  • Search
  • Time
  • People

Categories

Types of content, like in a H & M, or Bench.

Tasks

Goals users need to achieve

“Save, Loan, Invest, Get Help, Open an Account”

Search

If your site is very complex, or if it is mostly full of user-generated content, a search-based Architecture might make more sense, like YouTube.

Time

You can also design Information Architecture that changes with time.

The simplest version would be your inbox, where messages are displayed in the order they arrived.

People

Social Network, like Facebook

What is a Wireframe?

If we are the “architect”, wireframes are the blueprints.

What isn't a wireframe?

  1. Wireframes are not a basic sketch.
  2. Good wireframes take time.
  3. Wireframes aren't presented in phases
  4. Wireframes should be taken seriously.
  5. Wireframes are not meant for display.

A wireframe is a technical document, like the one from the previous slide. Lines, boxes, labels. Maybe a color or two. That’s it.

Wireframes are often compared to blueprints because they have a similar purpose.
